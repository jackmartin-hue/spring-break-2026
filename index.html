Where to Eat

The Food List

Naples
Da Michele
Pizza — The Pilgrimage
Founded 1870. Cash only. Two pizzas: marinara or margherita. No reservations. Arrive early. Everything else is noise.
Cossentina de Tre Santi
Pizza — Local Favorite
Beloved neighborhood spot, less tourist traffic than Da Michele. Perfect for a comparison round.
Culinary Backstreets Tour
Food Tour — Book Ahead
The best way to eat Naples. Guided deep-dive into street food, markets, and hidden spots. Book at culinarybackstreets.com — it sells out.
Naples Underground Tour
Culture + History
Ancient tunnels beneath the city — fascinating and eerie. A must-do. Book via napolisotterranea.org.
Spaccanapoli Street Food
Budget Eats — Anywhere
Walk and eat. Fried pizza, cuoppo (fried snacks), sfogliatelle pastry. Pizza slices from €2. You can't make a bad choice here.
Rome
Armando al Pantheon
Trattoria — Book Ahead
Family-run since 1961. Steps from the Pantheon. Order cacio e pepe or amatriciana. Reserve in advance — it's always full.
Al Pompieri
Trattoria — Jewish Ghetto
Inside a historic palazzo. Roman-Jewish classics including carciofi alla giudia (fried artichokes). Very local, very good.
E' passata la moretta
Daily Menu — Seasonal
Family-run, market-driven, changes daily. Eat whatever they're making — it will be exactly right.
Roscioli Pizza
Pizza al Taglio — Grab & Go
World-class Roman-style pizza by the slice. Great for lunch between sightseeing. Fast, cheap, excellent.
Forno in Campo de' Fiori
Bakery — Breakfast Slices
Right on the market square. Pizza bianca in the morning — this is what Romans eat for breakfast. Unmissable.
Cul de Sac
Wine Bar — Natural Wines
One of Rome's oldest wine bars, near Piazza Navona. Hundreds of bottles, great charcuterie and cheese. Perfect aperitivo stop.
Da Michele Vincenzo (Rome)
Pizza — Naples Import
The Naples Da Michele family's Rome outpost. Get your fix here if you need a final pizza before heading home.

Practical Advice

Traveler's Tips

💶
Money & Budget
Budget ~€50–70/day for food and incidentals. Italy is affordable if you avoid tourist-trap restaurants. Many smaller spots are cash-only, especially in Naples. Bring a Wise or Charles Schwab card for fee-free ATM withdrawals. Don't use airport currency exchange booths.
🚆
Getting Around
High-speed trains between Rome and Naples take 70 minutes — book online at trenitalia.com or italotreno.it at least a few days ahead for €20–35 fares. Circumvesuviana to Pompeii costs ~€3. Amalfi Coast: take SITA buses or ferries between towns — both cheap, both scenic, both crowded in peak season.
🏛️
Book Attractions Early
The Colosseum requires timed-entry tickets — book 2–3 weeks ahead at coopculture.it. Same for the Vatican Museums. Pompeii is more walk-up friendly. Naples Underground can be booked at napolisotterranea.org. Pre-booking = skipping massive lines = more pizza time.
🍝
Eating on a Budget
Sit-down lunch menus (menù del giorno) offer 2 courses + wine for €12–15. Aperitivo hour (6–8 PM) often includes free snacks with a drink. Espresso at the bar is €1–1.50 — never order it sitting down at a tourist café. Avoid any restaurant with photos on the menu near a major landmark.
📱
Staying Connected
Get a local Italian SIM at the airport (Tim, Vodafone IT, or Iliad) for ~€15–20 with plenty of data. Download Google Maps offline for Rome and Naples before you go. Citymapper works well in Rome. WhatsApp for communication — it's what everyone in Europe uses.
⚠️
Watch Out For
Pickpockets on the Rome Metro Line A near Termini and the Vatican. Cover shoulders and knees for entering churches. Amalfi Coast buses fill up — board early. The sit-down surcharge at cafés is real (espresso at the bar: €1.50; at a table: €4). And set four alarms for your 4 AM Naples wake-up. Not joking.
